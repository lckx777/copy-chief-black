# Sugarman's 30 Psychological Triggers - Complete Practical Checklist

> **Expert:** Joe Sugarman
> **Source:** Triggers (2005), Advertising Secrets of the Written Word, The Adweek Copywriting Handbook
> **Use:** After writing body copy. Score: count how many triggers are activated. Target: 8+ per 1000 words.
> **Rule:** Never apply triggers mechanically. They must be woven into the narrative so the reader FEELS their effect without seeing the mechanism.

## Part 1: The 30 Psychological Triggers

### Group 1: Emotional Triggers

These tap into the reader's feelings, desires, and internal states. They create the emotional
foundation that drives the purchase decision.

- [ ] **#1 Feeling of Involvement/Ownership** -- Make the reader participate mentally. Use "imagine," "picture yourself," "what would you do." Involvement creates psychological ownership before purchase. Vary the verb -- never use "imagine" more than 3 times. Place at opening, product intro, and close. Min 3x.

- [ ] **#2 Honesty** -- Admit a flaw, a limitation, or something against your selling interest. "I'll be honest -- this isn't for everyone." Every admission of weakness makes every claim of strength more believable. Once in first 20%, once near offer. Min 2x.

- [ ] **#3 Integrity** -- Deliver on EVERY promise made in copy. If headline says "3 reasons," deliver exactly 3. If you say "I'll explain in a moment," explain. Audit for unfulfilled commitments. Continuous.

- [ ] **#4 Credibility** -- Back claims with specific, verifiable proof. "Published in the Journal of Clinical Nutrition, Vol. 47, March 2024." Credibility gives the reader PERMISSION to believe. Every major claim. 4-6x.

- [ ] **#5 Value and Proof of Value** -- Demonstrate value before asking for money using concrete comparisons. "If you went to a specialist, you'd pay $300/hour." Show the math. Make the price feel low relative to value. Mid-copy + offer. 2x.

- [ ] **#6 Justify the Purchase** -- Give logical reasons to justify the emotional decision. Cost-per-day breakdown, comparison to alternatives, ROI calculation. "At $1.63/day, less than your coffee." Armor against buyer's remorse. 2-3x near offer.

- [ ] **#7 Greed** -- Stack value to 10x of asking price. Bonus layering, price anchoring, component stacking. "The bonuses alone are worth 5x what you're paying." Reader should feel they're STEALING. Concentrated in offer stack.

### Group 2: Authority & Expertise Triggers

- [ ] **#8 Establish Authority** -- Position through DEMONSTRATION, not assertion. Don't say "I'm an expert." Say "After testing 847 formulations over 6 years..." Show insider knowledge casually. Early + technical sections. 3-4x.

- [ ] **#9 Satisfaction Conviction** -- Strong guarantee + REASON for guarantee. "I can offer this because 97.3% never ask for a refund." The reason for the guarantee IS the proof. Guarantee section + PS. 2x.

- [ ] **#10 Nature of Product** -- Match copy tone to product nature. Luxury=elegant, tech=precise, health=warm authority, novelty=playful. Mismatched tone kills the sale silently. Continuous tonal constraint.

### Group 3: Timing & Relevance Triggers

- [ ] **#11 Current Fads** -- Connect to trending topics. "In the age of AI, nobody noticed that [insight]..." Hijack existing mental availability. Don't force connections. 1-2x in opening or bridge paragraph.

- [ ] **#12 Timing** -- Urgency tied to external event. "Tax season starts in 47 days. Every day costs $12.80 in missed deductions." Real external timing > manufactured timing. 1x in close. Must be believable.

### Group 4: Social & Belonging Triggers

- [ ] **#13 Sense of Belonging** -- "Join 12,847 people who already know this." Create named communities. Use insider language. Purchase = tribal initiation. 1-2x mid-copy and near CTA.

- [ ] **#14 Desire to Collect** -- Position as part of set/system. "This is Module 3 of 5." Incomplete sets create tension resolved by acquisition. 1x in offer section. Best for continuity/subscription.

- [ ] **#15 Curiosity** -- Plant unresolved questions. "There's a reason your doctor never mentioned this." Curiosity IS the slippery slope engine. Every 3-4 paragraphs. Min 5x. Most-used trigger.

- [ ] **#16 Sense of Urgency** -- Real reason to act NOW. "This batch: 340 units. Next batch: April." Urgency must be REAL or deeply plausible. Fake urgency destroys trust. 1-2x in close only.

- [ ] **#17 Fear** -- What they LOSE by not acting. "Every day you wait, the damage compounds." Loss aversion is 2x more powerful than hope of gain. Specific + credible. Always follow with path out. 1-2x problem + 1x close.

- [ ] **#18 Instant Gratification** -- What they get IMMEDIATELY. "Within 5 minutes, the complete system is in your inbox." Bridges "I want it" to "I'll act now." 1x after CTA/price reveal.

- [ ] **#19 Exclusivity/Rarity** -- "Not sold in stores. Not on Amazon." "We only accept 200/quarter." Reader feels selected, not targeted. 1x near offer. Combine with Belonging (#13).

- [ ] **#20 Simplicity** -- Complex ideas explained so a 12-year-old understands. "Think of your metabolism like a furnace." Analogies. Kitchen-table language. Complexity = inaction. 3-4x when explaining mechanism/product.

- [ ] **#21 Human Relationships** -- Personal connection seller-to-buyer. "My wife asked why I'm giving this away at this price..." Personal details, shared experiences, genuine warmth. Tonal trigger throughout. Especially opening + close.

- [ ] **#22 Storytelling** -- Carry information inside narrative. "It started in a small lab in Zurich..." Stories bypass skepticism because defenses are down. Major story in first 30%, mini-stories throughout. 3-5x total.

- [ ] **#23 Mental Engagement** -- Force the reader to THINK. "Quick math: 45 min/day x 365 = 274 hours. What would you DO with 274 hours?" Active thinking = engagement = sliding. Every 500 words. Min 4x.

- [ ] **#24 Guilt** -- Moral obligation to self/family/future. "You owe it to your family to at least try." Powerful but dangerous -- light touch only. Position purchase as responsible choice. 1x max near close.

- [ ] **#25 Specificity** -- Exact numbers, names, dates, locations. Not "thousands" -- "12,847 in 23 countries." Non-round numbers feel measured; round feel estimated. Every major claim. Min 6x. Most important trust trigger.

- [ ] **#26 Familiarity** -- Connect unknown to known. "Think of it as a Fitbit for your brain." Reduces perceived risk. When the unknown maps onto the known, it becomes approachable. 1-2x at product/mechanism intro.

- [ ] **#27 Hope** -- Realistic, achievable positive future. "Margaret was 62. Three months later, her doctor called back in -- not because something was wrong." Hope + evidence. Hope without evidence = delusion. 1x mid-copy, 1x close. Min 2x.

- [ ] **#28 Pattern Interrupt** -- Break expected rhythm. "STOP. Read that again." "Everything I've told you? Forget it." Brain is a prediction machine; breaking prediction spikes attention. 2-3x at transitions and before key revelations.

- [ ] **#29 Linking** -- Connect product to something already valued. "The same principle [admired company] uses." Borrowed equity transfers automatically. Association > explanation. 2-3x in intro and proof.

- [ ] **#30 Consistency/Harmonize** -- Build chain of mental "yeses." "You've noticed [obvious truth]. Right?" Each yes makes the next easier. By the sale, reader has said yes 10+ times. 5-8x throughout, easy yeses first.

## Part 3: Slippery Slope Audit (10 Checkpoints)

Run this audit on EVERY long-form piece before delivery. Each checkpoint
is a potential leak in the slope. One leak can lose the reader.

- [ ] **SL-1: First Sentence Test** -- Is the first sentence 5-8 words or fewer? Is it impossible to read without reading the second? Examples that pass: "It was an accident." "I lied." "She said three words." If the first sentence requires context, background, or effort = FAIL.

- [ ] **SL-2: Paragraph Ending Audit** -- Read ONLY the last sentence of every paragraph. Does each one create forward pull? Does each one make you NEED to read the next paragraph? Check for exit ramps: paragraphs that resolve completely, summarize, or feel "finished." Every exit ramp = a reader lost.

- [ ] **SL-3: Active Seed Count** -- At any given point in the copy, how many unresolved open loops exist? Target: 1-4 active seeds. More than 4 = confusion. Zero = no momentum. Track on a separate sheet: PLANTED at line X, RESOLVED at line Y.

- [ ] **SL-4: Seed Harvest Audit** -- Is every planted seed resolved? Check that no seed is orphaned (planted but never harvested). Orphan seeds destroy trust retroactively. The reader feels manipulated.

- [ ] **SL-5: Information Rationing Test** -- Does each paragraph reveal exactly ONE new piece of information? Look for information dumps: paragraphs with 3+ data points, feature lists, or dense technical explanations without emotional anchors between them. One fact, one feeling.

- [ ] **SL-6: Resolution Delay Check** -- Is the biggest question/mystery saved for the final 20% of copy? The reader should be sliding toward the answer throughout. If the biggest reveal comes at 50%, the second half has no gravity.

- [ ] **SL-7: Comfortable Stop Test** -- Read the copy and mark every point where you could comfortably STOP reading without feeling you missed something. Target: ZERO comfortable stops before the CTA. Each comfortable stop = a structural failure.

- [ ] **SL-8: Transition Invisibility Test** -- Read only the last sentence of each section + first sentence of the next. Do they flow naturally? Would you notice the section break if it weren't marked? If the transition is visible, it's a speed bump on the slope.

- [ ] **SL-9: Velocity Variation Check** -- Does the pacing vary? Short paragraphs for speed/urgency, longer ones for immersion. Uniform pacing = monotone = reader drifts. Check that no more than 3 consecutive paragraphs have similar length.

- [ ] **SL-10: Read-Aloud Test** -- Read the entire piece aloud. Note where you stumble, pause, or lose rhythm. Every stumble is where a reader would disengage. If you can read it smoothly from start to CTA without wanting to stop, the slope works.

**Slope Score:**

| Checkpoints Passed | Rating | Action |
|--------------------|--------|--------|
| 10/10 | AIRTIGHT | Ship it |
| 8-9/10 | SOLID | Fix the 1-2 leaks, then ship |
| 6-7/10 | LEAKY | Structural revision needed on failed checkpoints |
| Below 6/10 | BROKEN | Major rewrite. The slope has too many exit ramps. |

## Part 4: Buying Environment Setup Checklist

The first 20% of copy creates the ENVIRONMENT, not the argument.
Run this checklist on the opening section before moving to the body.

### The 4 Pillars (ALL must be present)

- [ ] **BE-1: Trust Established** -- Have you admitted one honest limitation, flaw, or uncomfortable truth in the opening? The reader must feel "this person is not trying to manipulate me" before any selling begins. Check: is there an admission that goes AGAINST your selling interest?

- [ ] **BE-2: Rapport Built** -- Does the opening mirror the reader's world? Their language? Their frustrations? Their daily experience? Check: would the reader nod and think "this person gets me" within the first 3 paragraphs?

- [ ] **BE-3: Expertise Demonstrated** -- Have you shown (not claimed) knowledge? Through casual technical detail, insider knowledge, or depth that only an expert would have? Check: does the reader think "this person knows what they're talking about" WITHOUT you saying "I'm an expert"?

- [ ] **BE-4: Expectation Set** -- Does the reader know what kind of experience they're in for? Is there a frame-setting statement like "I'm going to show you something. But first..." or "What I'm about to share changed everything I thought I knew about..."? Check: does the reader feel comfortable and oriented?

### Environment Match

- [ ] **BE-5: Tone Matches Product** -- Is the copy's personality consistent with the product's nature? Luxury demands elegance. Health demands warm authority. Tech demands clean precision. Novelty demands playful energy. Check: would this tone feel wrong if paired with the product?

- [ ] **BE-6: Consistency Maintained** -- Is the voice/tone the same from first word to last? No shifts from casual to corporate, from warm to clinical, from personal to institutional. Check: read the first paragraph and the last -- do they sound like the same person?

### Harmonize Pattern

- [ ] **BE-7: Yes Pattern Started** -- Has the reader said "yes" mentally at least 3 times before the product is mentioned? Through obvious truths, shared experiences, or undeniable observations. Check: count the statements a reasonable reader would agree with in the first 20%.

### Product Delay

- [ ] **BE-8: Product Name Absent** -- Does the product name appear in the first 30% of copy? If yes, it's too early. The buying environment is not about the product. It's about establishing the conditions where the product will be welcomed.

- [ ] **BE-9: No Selling Detected** -- Could the first 20% be read without the reader feeling they're being sold to? If a skeptic would think "here comes the pitch" at any point in the opening, the environment is not set.

- [ ] **BE-10: Pleasant Experience** -- Would someone who has NO interest in buying this product still find the opening enjoyable, interesting, or valuable to read? The best environments are genuinely entertaining or educational, independent of the sale.

**Environment Score:**

| Checkpoints Passed | Rating | Action |
|--------------------|--------|--------|
| 10/10 | PREMIUM ENVIRONMENT | Reader is primed. Proceed to body. |
| 8-9/10 | GOOD ENVIRONMENT | Minor adjustment, then proceed |
| 6-7/10 | THIN ENVIRONMENT | Strengthen the missing pillars before continuing |
| Below 6/10 | NO ENVIRONMENT | The reader hits the pitch cold. Rewrite the opening entirely. |

## Part 5: Curiosity Seed Planting Guide

A step-by-step operational guide for engineering curiosity throughout
long-form copy. Use this DURING writing, not just for audit.

### Step 1: Identify Your Seeds Before Writing

Before writing a single word, plan 4-6 seeds:

| Seed # | Type | What It Teases | When Planted | When Resolved |
|--------|------|---------------|-------------|---------------|
| A | [Major] | [Core mystery/mechanism] | First 10% | 60-75% |
| B | [Secondary] | [Surprising data point] | 10-20% | 25-40% |
| C | [Secondary] | [Expert discovery] | 15-25% | 40-60% |
| D | [Bridge] | [Objection anticipation] | 35-50% | 55-70% |
| E | [Purchase] | [Can only be resolved by buying] | 75-90% | CTA |

### Step 2: Seed Planting Phrases (by type)

**Time Seeds** (promise resolution later):
- "I'll explain exactly why in a moment."
- "The answer to that is coming. But you need context first."
- "I'll get to the most important part shortly."

**Reversal Seeds** (subvert expectations):
- "The reason will surprise you -- but not as much as what happened next."
- "What I'm about to show you contradicts everything you've been told."
- "The obvious answer is wrong. The real answer is stranger."

**Gating Seeds** (create prerequisites):
- "There's a second part to this that changes everything. But first..."
- "Before I can explain how this works, you need to understand one thing."
- "The solution only makes sense once you see the real problem."

**Callback Seeds** (reference earlier plants):
- "Remember the number I mentioned earlier? Here's why it matters."
- "That detail from the beginning? It's about to make perfect sense."
- "Earlier I said [X]. Now you'll see why."

**Exclusion Seeds** (create in-group curiosity):
- "Most people miss this completely."
- "What I'm about to share, I've never told publicly."
- "Only the people who read this far will understand."

**Purchase Seeds** (resolve only through buying):
- "The third ingredient is the key to everything. It's inside [product]."
- "The complete protocol -- all 7 steps -- is in [product]."
- "The specific calibration for YOUR situation is what [product] calculates."

### Step 3: Resolution Phrases

When harvesting seeds, use callback language:
- "Remember when I said [seed]? Well, here's why..."
- "Now you understand why [seed reference] matters."
- "This is what I was referring to earlier when I mentioned [seed]."
- "The answer to the question I raised in the beginning is this:"

### Step 4: Seed Tracking Template

Use during writing to ensure no orphan seeds:

```
SEED TRACKER
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Seed A: [description]
  Planted: paragraph ___  |  Resolved: paragraph ___  |  Status: [ ]

Seed B: [description]
  Planted: paragraph ___  |  Resolved: paragraph ___  |  Status: [ ]

Seed C: [description]
  Planted: paragraph ___  |  Resolved: paragraph ___  |  Status: [ ]

Seed D: [description]
  Planted: paragraph ___  |  Resolved: paragraph ___  |  Status: [ ]

Seed E (Purchase): [description]
  Planted: paragraph ___  |  Resolved: CTA           |  Status: [ ]

ACTIVE SEED COUNT AT ANY POINT: ___  (target: 1-4, never 0 or 5+)
ORPHAN SEEDS: ___  (target: 0)
─────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### Common Seed Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Planting too many at once | Confusion, not curiosity | Max 2 new seeds per section |
| Resolving too quickly | No tension builds | Let seeds breathe for 500+ words |
| Resolving the biggest seed at 50% | Second half has no gravity | Save seed A for the 60-75% mark |
| Generic seeds | "You'll be surprised" creates zero tension | Always make seeds SPECIFIC |
| Identical seed structures | Reader detects the pattern | Vary: time, reversal, gating, callback |
| Zero seeds after 60% | No pull toward CTA | Plant at least one seed in the final third |
| Never resolving a seed | Trust destruction | Every seed must harvest. No exceptions. |
