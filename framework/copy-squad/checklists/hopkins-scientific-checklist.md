## Section 1: Scientific Method Application Audit

Before scoring the copy itself, verify that the PROCESS followed
scientific advertising principles. Copy produced without scientific
method is copy produced by accident -- and accidents do not scale.

### 1A. Pre-Production Scientific Standards

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Target audience DEFINED before writing** -- A specific person with specific needs was identified. Not "everyone" or "women 25-54." A PERSON. | | Hopkins studied the consumer before writing a word. "The advertising man studies the consumer. He tries to place himself in the position of the buyer." Writing to "everyone" sells to no one. |
| 2 | [ ] **Product studied exhaustively** -- The writer can describe the manufacturing process, the key ingredients/components, and the mechanism of action without consulting reference material. | | Hopkins toured factories, interviewed chemists, studied processes. He knew Schlitz's brewing process better than most Schlitz employees. You cannot sell what you do not understand at a molecular level. |
| 3 | [ ] **Competitor analysis completed** -- At least 5 competitor ads were studied. Their claims, their proof, their offers were documented. Gaps and opportunities were identified. | | Hopkins always knew what the market was saying before he said anything. The Schlitz breakthrough came from knowing that EVERY brewer claimed "pure" -- and none explained it. |
| 4 | [ ] **Success metrics DEFINED before writing** -- Specific, measurable goals (cost-per-customer, conversion rate, AOV) were set BEFORE the first word was written. | | "Almost any question can be answered by a test campaign." But only if you define what answer you are looking for. Without pre-defined metrics, you cannot evaluate results. |
| 5 | [ ] **Test plan EXISTS** -- At minimum, the headline, the offer, and the CTA have A/B test variants prepared. Tracking infrastructure is ready. | | Hopkins never launched without a test plan. Split-run newspapers were his laboratory. No test plan = no science = no Hopkins approval. |
| 6 | [ ] **VOC/Customer language collected** -- Real quotes from real customers were gathered and used to inform the copy's language, promises, and proof points. | | Hopkins interviewed "hundreds of possible customers" and sent salesmen door-to-door before writing a single ad. The customer's language IS the copy's language. |

## Section 2: Scoring Matrix (100 Points)

### 2A. Testability (20 points)

Every major element of the copy must be TESTABLE -- meaning a
variation can be created, measured against the original, and the
winner determined by sales data (not opinion).

| Criterion | 0 pts | 5 pts | 10 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|--------|
| Headline testable? | No alternatives prepared | 2-3 variations | 5+ variations with test plan |
| Offer elements testable? | Single version | Key elements identified for test | Full A/B matrix defined |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Headline has 5+ testable variations** -- Each variation tests a different angle, promise, or structure. Not just word swaps. | | Hopkins tested dozens of headlines per campaign. The headline alone can change results by 5-10x. Five variations is the minimum, not the ideal. |
| 2 | [ ] **CTA has at least 3 variations** -- Different action words, urgency levels, or framing ("Get yours" vs "Claim your free sample" vs "See the evidence"). | | The CTA is the moment of conversion. Testing only one CTA is leaving money on the table. Hopkins tracked every coupon variation. |
| 3 | [ ] **Offer structure has test plan** -- Price points, bonuses, guarantees, or trial periods have alternatives ready to test. | | "The severest test of any advertising is in mail order sales." The offer IS the product in the prospect's mind. Test the offer as rigorously as you test the copy. |
| 4 | [ ] **Success metrics defined BEFORE launch** -- Cost-per-customer, conversion rate, ROAS target documented. Not "we'll see how it does." | | A test without pre-defined success metrics is not a test -- it is a hope. Hopkins knew his target cost-per-customer before the first ad ran. |
| 5 | [ ] **Tracking parameters in place** -- UTMs, pixels, key-codes, or equivalent tracking on every CTA and every landing page variant. | | Hopkins key-coded every coupon. If you cannot trace the response to the specific ad that generated it, you are running advertising blind. |
| 6 | [ ] **Control version identified** -- A baseline exists (previous winner, competitor benchmark, or industry standard) against which new copy will be measured. | | Testing without a control is not testing. You need a baseline to measure improvement against. Even a "first test" needs a benchmark hypothesis. |
| 7 | [ ] **Sample size calculated** -- The test will run long enough and to a large enough audience to produce statistically meaningful results. | | A 2-day test with 47 responses is not science. Hopkins tested in representative markets for representative durations before drawing conclusions. |

**Score: ___/20**

### 2B. Specificity (20 points)

Hopkins proved that specific claims outperform general claims in
every measured test. This section verifies that the copy is SPECIFIC
enough to trigger the "this must be real" neurological response.

| Criterion | 0 pts | 5 pts | 10 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|--------|
| Numbers specific? | Round/vague | Some specific | All non-round, precise |
| Claims quantified? | General | Partially | Every claim has a number |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Zero round numbers** -- No "100 people", "50% better", "thousands". All numbers are precise: "2,847 patients", "87.3% improvement", "$347.82". | | Round numbers feel INVENTED. Non-round numbers feel MEASURED. The brain flags round numbers as approximations and distrusts them. |
| 2 | [ ] **Named individuals with details** -- At least one named person with city, age, or profession appears. Not "a doctor" but "Dr. Heinrich Mueller, 47, Chief of Audiology at Heidelberg." | | Named individuals are verifiable. Anonymous claims are deniable. Specificity of identity shifts perception from "marketing claim" to "real story." |
| 3 | [ ] **Mechanism named with specific terminology** -- The process, ingredient, or method is NAMED and DESCRIBED. Not "our technology" but "3-stage CoQ10 absorption protocol." | | Hopkins named the Pepsodent film, described the Schlitz filtration. Unnamed mechanisms sound generic. Named mechanisms sound proprietary and specific. |
| 4 | [ ] **Results quantified with timeframe** -- Every benefit claim includes a number AND a duration. Not "lose weight" but "lost 23 lbs in 87 days." | | A benefit without a timeframe is a wish. A benefit with a timeframe is a promise. Hopkins always included measurable outcomes within specific periods. |
| 5 | [ ] **Authority sources named** -- Institutions, studies, publications, experts cited by NAME, not by category. Not "studies show" but "a 2019 study by Dr. Mueller at Heidelberg University." | | Unnamed authorities carry zero weight. Named authorities carry the weight of their reputation. If you cannot name the source, the prospect cannot believe the claim. |
| 6 | [ ] **Process described in factory-tour detail** -- At least one section describes HOW the product works or is made, with specific steps, temperatures, durations, or quantities. | | The Schlitz breakthrough. "Pure beer" meant nothing. "Aged for 1,173 hours, bottles sterilized 4 times, water from wells 4,000 feet deep" meant everything. |
| 7 | [ ] **Competitor-proof specificity** -- The specific details in this copy could NOT be used by a competitor without modification. The details are PROPRIETARY to this product. | | If a competitor can steal your copy word-for-word and it still works for their product, your copy is not specific enough. Hopkins' copy was anchored to the SPECIFIC product. |
| 8 | [ ] **Sensory detail present** -- At least one claim includes a sensory element (visual, tactile, auditory, olfactory). Not "feels better" but "the tingling sensation in the first 30 seconds." | | Pepsodent's tingling was the sensory detail that anchored the reward loop. Sensory details are processed by the brain as EXPERIENCES, not claims. |

**Score: ___/20**

### 2C. Reason-Why Verification (15 points)

Every claim must have a REASON attached. "Better" is not enough.
"Better BECAUSE [specific mechanism]" is the Hopkins standard.

| Criterion | 0 pts | 5 pts | 8 pts | 15 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| Reason-why density? | Claims without reasons | Some reasons given | Most claims supported | Every claim has a "because" |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Every major claim has a "because"** -- For each benefit claimed, there is an explicit reason WHY it works, HOW it is produced, or WHAT causes the effect. | | "The weight of an argument may often be doubled by making it specific." A claim with a reason is twice as persuasive as a claim alone. |
| 2 | [ ] **The mechanism is EXPLAINED, not just named** -- The product's method of action is described in enough detail that the prospect understands the process. Not just "uses CoQ10" but "CoQ10 enhances mitochondrial energy production in inner ear hair cells." | | Hopkins did not just claim Schlitz was pure -- he explained the 4x sterilization, the artesian wells, the filtered-air rooms. The explanation IS the proof. |
| 3 | [ ] **Price is justified** -- The copy explains WHY the product costs what it costs. The reason may be quality of ingredients, research investment, manufacturing process, or limited availability. | | Hopkins never apologized for price. He explained why the price was JUSTIFIED. "Cheapness is not an appeal." The prospect must feel the price is fair, not just low. |
| 4 | [ ] **The offer limitation is EXPLAINED** -- If scarcity is claimed (limited quantity, deadline), there is a genuine REASON for the limitation. Not fake urgency. | | Hopkins used real scarcity backed by real reasons. "We can only produce X per month because of [manufacturing constraint]." Fake scarcity insults intelligence. |
| 5 | [ ] **The guarantee has a reason** -- If a guarantee is offered, the copy explains WHY the company can offer it (confidence in the product, low return rates, specific test results). | | A guarantee without a reason feels like a trick. A guarantee WITH a reason feels like confidence. "We offer 180 days because in our testing, 94% of users saw results by day 60." |
| 6 | [ ] **Objections are addressed with reasons** -- The copy anticipates at least 3 major objections and provides logical reasons to overcome each one. | | Hopkins answered objections before they arose. "You may wonder why..." followed by a REASON. Unanswered objections become reasons NOT to buy. |

**Score: ___/15**

### 2D. Accountability & Tracking (15 points)

Every piece of copy must be traceable to a measurable result.
Hopkins' coupon mentality applied to every element.

| Criterion | 0 pts | 5 pts | 8 pts | 15 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| Trackable? | No tracking | Basic pixels | UTM params | Full attribution model |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Every CTA has tracking** -- Each call-to-action leads to a trackable endpoint (UTM-coded URL, unique landing page, key-coded form). | | Hopkins key-coded every coupon. If you cannot identify which CTA generated which response, you cannot optimize. |
| 2 | [ ] **Revenue attributable to this specific copy** -- There is a mechanism to calculate how much revenue this specific piece of copy generated (not "the campaign" but THIS copy). | | Hopkins calculated cost-per-customer per ad, per headline, per publication. The copy must stand on its own financial merit. |
| 3 | [ ] **A/B test infrastructure ready** -- The technical infrastructure for split-testing is in place BEFORE launch (multiple landing pages, email variations, or ad set configurations). | | Testing is not optional. It is the foundation. If the infrastructure is not ready at launch, the first impressions are wasted (no data collected). |
| 4 | [ ] **Conversion funnel mapped with measurement points** -- Every step from impression to sale has a measurement point. Where the prospect drops off can be identified. | | Hopkins tracked not just the final conversion but every step. Which ad drove the inquiry. Which inquiry became a sale. Where the losses occurred. |
| 5 | [ ] **ROI calculable post-campaign** -- Total spend, total revenue, cost-per-customer, and ROAS can be calculated after the campaign runs. | | "Treat it as a salesman. Force it to justify itself." An ad whose ROI cannot be calculated has not been treated as a salesman. It has been treated as a decoration. |
| 6 | [ ] **Cost-per-customer target SET** -- A specific, pre-defined maximum cost-per-customer exists. If the copy exceeds this cost, it fails regardless of other metrics. | | Hopkins knew his cost-per-customer before the first ad ran. Without a target, you cannot distinguish a winning campaign from a losing one that "looks successful." |
| 7 | [ ] **Losing variation kill criteria defined** -- There is a pre-set threshold below which a variation will be killed (e.g., "If CTR is below 1.5% after 1,000 impressions, kill"). | | Hopkins killed losing ads quickly and without sentiment. Lingering with losing copy wastes money. The kill criteria must be set BEFORE the test, not decided after. |

**Score: ___/15**

### 2E. Economy & Completeness (10 points)

Every word must earn its place. But the story must be COMPLETE.
The Hopkins standard is not "short copy" -- it is "lean copy that
tells the full story."

| Criterion | 0 pts | 3 pts | 7 pts | 10 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| Word efficiency? | Bloated | Some filler | Lean | Every word sells AND full story told |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Zero filler sentences** -- Every sentence advances the sale. No sentences exist purely for rhythm, transition, or "flow" without selling content. | | "In mail order advertising, every word costs money." Even in digital, every word costs attention. Filler is theft from the reader and the advertiser. |
| 2 | [ ] **No repeated ideas in different words** -- Each paragraph introduces NEW information or a NEW argument. No concept appears twice in different phrasing. | | Repetition feels like padding. If the idea was worth saying, it was worth saying ONCE -- clearly and completely. If it needs repeating, the first version was unclear. |
| 3 | [ ] **Qualifiers and hedges eliminated** -- No "somewhat", "relatively", "kind of", "sort of", "perhaps", "may", "might", "could possibly". | | Qualifiers signal uncertainty. Uncertainty kills conviction. Hopkins' copy was CERTAIN because his claims were TESTED. If you are not certain enough to state it plainly, do not state it at all. |
| 4 | [ ] **Complete story told** -- Every major benefit is stated. Every major objection is addressed. Every proof element is presented. The reader who finishes has NO unanswered questions. | | "The more you tell, the more you sell" -- but ONLY if everything you tell is relevant. Completeness is not length. It is thoroughness. |
| 5 | [ ] **Read aloud without stumbling** -- The entire copy reads smoothly when spoken aloud. No sentences require re-reading. No phrases cause the tongue to trip. | | If the writer stumbles reading it, the reader will stumble reading it. And the reader will not re-read. They will leave. |
| 6 | [ ] **Mail-order standard applied** -- If you were paying per word to print and mail this, would you keep every word? If not, cut. | | The mail-order test is the ultimate economy filter. Money per word forces discipline. Apply this standard even to digital copy. |

**Score: ___/10**

### 2F. Clarity (10 points)

The prospect should not need to re-read, decode, or interpret.
The meaning should be OBVIOUS on first reading to someone with
no special knowledge of the topic.

| Criterion | 0 pts | 3 pts | 7 pts | 10 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| Understandable? | Confusing | Some jargon | Clear | Crystal -- 12-year-old understands |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **No jargon without immediate explanation** -- Every technical term is followed by a plain-language explanation in the same sentence or the next. | | Hopkins wrote for the BUYER, not for the manufacturer. Technical language impresses experts but loses customers. |
| 2 | [ ] **Core message statable in one sentence** -- The central promise of the copy can be expressed in a single, clear sentence of 20 words or fewer. | | If the writer cannot state the core message in one sentence, the reader certainly cannot. And a confused reader does not buy. |
| 3 | [ ] **Scan-readable** -- Subheads, bullets, and key phrases tell the story independently. A reader who only scans subheads gets the full promise. | | Hopkins understood that most people scan before reading. The scan must sell. If the subheads alone do not create desire, the body copy will never be read. |
| 4 | [ ] **No ambiguous pronoun references** -- Every "it", "this", "that", "they" has a clear, unambiguous antecedent. | | Ambiguity creates confusion. Confusion creates doubt. Doubt kills sales. Every pronoun must point to exactly one referent. |
| 5 | [ ] **Logical flow** -- Each paragraph follows naturally from the previous one. The reader never asks "Wait, why are we talking about THIS now?" | | Hopkins' copy was structured like a sales conversation: problem, cause, solution, proof, offer, action. Each step follows logically. Disordered copy loses the reader. |

**Score: ___/10**

### 2G. Service Orientation (5 points)

The ad must SERVE the reader first. It must provide genuine value,
useful information, or a meaningful offer before asking for anything.

| Criterion | 0 pts | 2 pts | 5 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|
| Service-first? | Pure sales pitch | Some information given | Ad genuinely helps the reader even without purchase |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **The ad provides useful information** -- The reader learns something valuable from the ad itself, independent of whether they buy. | | "The best ads are based entirely on service. They offer wanted information." An ad that only TAKES (attention, time) without GIVING (information, insight) violates the Hopkins principle. |
| 2 | [ ] **The ad does not open with a direct sales pitch** -- The first 3-5 sentences are informative, educational, or story-driven. Not "BUY NOW!" | | Hopkins: "The best ads ask no one to buy." The ask comes AFTER the service. Leading with a pitch triggers resistance. Leading with service builds trust. |
| 3 | [ ] **A trial or sample is offered** -- The prospect can experience the product at zero or minimal risk before full commitment. | | "The product itself is its own best salesman." Hopkins gave Pepsodent samples, Van Camp's samples, Schlitz information. Let the product prove itself. |
| 4 | [ ] **The tone is helpful, not pushy** -- The overall voice feels like an informed friend sharing a discovery, not a salesman closing a deal. | | "Successful salesmen are rarely good talkers. They are plain and sincere men." The ad should feel like advice, not pressure. |

**Score: ___/5**

### 2H. Preemptive Claim Identification (5 points)

Does the copy PREEMPT the competition by describing something
common as if it were proprietary? This is the Schlitz strategy.

| Criterion | 0 pts | 2 pts | 5 pts |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|
| Preemptive power? | No preemptive elements | Some process description | Full factory-tour preemption |

**Detailed Checks:**

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **At least one industry-standard process is described as if proprietary** -- A step in the product's creation that competitors also do, but have not EXPLAINED to the public, is described in specific detail. | | The entire Schlitz campaign. The process was standard. The explanation was revolutionary. First to explain = first to own in the consumer's mind. |
| 2 | [ ] **The description uses factory-tour language** -- The reader can VISUALIZE the process. Specific details (temperatures, durations, quantities, locations) create a mental image. | | "Plate-glass rooms where beer cools in filtered air" -- you can SEE it. Abstract descriptions ("advanced process") create no mental image and therefore no belief. |
| 3 | [ ] **No competitor has already claimed this in the market** -- The specific angle or detail has been verified as unclaimed by competitors. | | A preemptive claim only works if you are FIRST. If a competitor has already described the same process, you need a different angle. Research before claiming. |
| 4 | [ ] **The preemptive claim supports the main mechanism** -- The factory-tour detail is not random; it directly supports the product's unique mechanism or key benefit. | | Schlitz's process details all supported the claim of purity. Irrelevant process details are interesting but do not sell. Every detail must connect to the promise. |

## Section 3: Hopkins Test Plan Audit

This section verifies that the TEST PLAN -- not the copy itself --
meets scientific advertising standards. Copy without a test plan is
a hypothesis without an experiment.

### 3A. Headline Testing Readiness

| # | Check | Pass? | Details |
|---|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | [ ] **Minimum 5 headline variations written** -- Each tests a genuinely different angle (not just word swaps). | |
| 2 | [ ] **Headlines categorized by type** -- News, Question, How-To, Warning, Story, Benefit, Curiosity. At least 3 types represented. | |
| 3 | [ ] **Each headline includes target audience selector** -- The headline speaks to a SPECIFIC reader, not to everyone. | |
| 4 | [ ] **Each headline is self-contained** -- The headline alone communicates the core promise without requiring body copy. | |
| 5 | [ ] **Specificity present in at least 3 headlines** -- At least 3 variations include specific numbers, names, or mechanisms. | |
| 6 | [ ] **Split-test methodology defined** -- HOW the test will run (which platforms, which audiences, what sample sizes, what duration). | |
| 7 | [ ] **Winner criteria defined** -- What metric determines the winner (CTR? Sales? Cost-per-customer?) and what margin of victory is required. | |

### 3B. Offer Testing Readiness

| # | Check | Pass? | Details |
|---|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | [ ] **At least 2 offer structures ready** -- Different combinations of price, bonuses, guarantees, or trial periods. | |
| 2 | [ ] **Offer elements isolated for testing** -- Each test changes ONE element (price OR bonus OR guarantee), not multiple. | |
| 3 | [ ] **Revenue-per-customer calculated for each offer** -- Not just conversion rate, but REVENUE per 1,000 impressions or per 1,000 visitors. | |
| 4 | [ ] **Sampling/trial option included** -- At least one offer variant includes a free or low-risk trial (the Hopkins sampling principle). | |
| 5 | [ ] **Guarantee tested** -- At least two guarantee variants (duration, conditions, or framing) are prepared for testing. | |

### 3C. Market Testing Readiness

| # | Check | Pass? | Details |
|---|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | [ ] **Test market selected** -- A representative market has been chosen for the initial test (not the full audience). | |
| 2 | [ ] **Budget allocated for testing BEFORE scaling** -- A specific percentage of budget is reserved for testing, separate from the scaling budget. | |
| 3 | [ ] **Scale criteria defined** -- What results in the test market trigger the decision to scale (cost-per-customer target, conversion rate floor). | |
| 4 | [ ] **Kill criteria defined** -- What results trigger the decision to stop the campaign and rewrite (max cost-per-customer, minimum conversion floor). | |
| 5 | [ ] **Timeline set** -- How long the test will run before results are evaluated. Not "until we feel good about it." | |

## Section 4: Reason-Why Copy Deep Audit

This section goes deeper than Section 2C. It evaluates the QUALITY
and COMPLETENESS of the reason-why structure throughout the copy.

### 4A. Claim-Reason Mapping

For each major claim in the copy, map the corresponding reason.
If any claim lacks a reason, the copy fails this section.

| # | Claim (from copy) | Reason Given? | Reason Quality (1-10) | Notes |
|---|-------------------|---------------|----------------------|-------|
| 1 | [claim 1] | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | ___/10 | |
| 2 | [claim 2] | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | ___/10 | |
| 3 | [claim 3] | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | ___/10 | |
| 4 | [claim 4] | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | ___/10 | |
| 5 | [claim 5] | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | ___/10 | |

**Reason Quality Scale:**
- 10: Verifiable, specific, mechanical ("CoQ10 enhances mitochondrial ATP production in cochlear hair cells")
- 8-9: Specific and logical ("4x sterilization kills 99.97% of bacteria")
- 6-7: Logical but could be more specific ("Our filtration removes impurities")
- 4-5: Generic reason ("High-quality ingredients")
- 1-3: No real reason ("Trust us" / "We're the best")

**Minimum: All claims have reasons. Average reason quality >= 7.**

### 4B. Mechanism Explanation Depth

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Root cause identified** -- The copy explains WHAT causes the problem, not just that the problem exists. | | Hopkins' Pepsodent: "mucin plaques" (the film) was the cause. Without naming the cause, the solution feels arbitrary. |
| 2 | [ ] **Solution mechanism described** -- The copy explains HOW the product addresses the root cause. The process, not just the result. | | Schlitz: not just "pure" but HOW purity is achieved (wells, sterilization, filtration, aging). The process IS the proof. |
| 3 | [ ] **Mechanism is NAMED** -- The mechanism has a specific, memorable name (not "our process" but "the 4-Stage Filtration Protocol"). | | Named mechanisms feel proprietary. Unnamed mechanisms feel generic. Hopkins named the film, the process, the ingredients. |
| 4 | [ ] **Mechanism explanation is accessible** -- A non-expert can understand the mechanism after one reading. Technical terms are translated. | | Hopkins wrote about chemistry, brewing, and dentistry for the general public. If the mechanism explanation confuses, it alienates. |
| 5 | [ ] **Mechanism connects to BENEFIT** -- The explanation of HOW it works connects directly to WHAT the reader gets. Process -> Outcome. | | Every Schlitz process detail connected to "purity." Every Pepsodent detail connected to "beautiful teeth." Disconnected mechanisms are irrelevant. |

## Section 6: Service Orientation Deep Audit

Does the copy genuinely SERVE the reader? Or does it only serve
the advertiser? Hopkins' highest-performing ads were those that
offered the most SERVICE to the reader.

| Copy Section | Gives Value? | Asks for Action? | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|------------------|-------|

| Problem section | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | |
| Mechanism section | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | |
| Proof section | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | |
| Offer section | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | |
| CTA section | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | [ ] Yes / [ ] No | |

**Hopkins Standard:** Value should be given in at LEAST the first 4 sections
before any significant ask. The reader should feel served, not sold.

### 6B. Sample/Trial Offering

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **A trial or sample is offered** -- Free trial, money-back guarantee, free chapter, free consultation, or demo available. | | Hopkins: "The product is its own best salesman." Risk-free trial reduces the barrier to first purchase. |
| 2 | [ ] **The trial is prominently featured** -- Not buried in fine print but highlighted as a key element of the offer. | | If the trial exists but the prospect does not see it, it does not exist. Hopkins put the free sample coupon front and center. |
| 3 | [ ] **The trial requires some effort from the prospect** -- Not given freely to everyone, but requires a coupon clip, form fill, or demonstration of interest. | | Hopkins sampled only INTERESTED prospects. Unqualified samples are waste. The effort required filters for genuine interest. |
| 4 | [ ] **The trial period is long enough to experience the benefit** -- The prospect has enough time to form a habit or see results. | | Pepsodent's 10-day trial was enough to form the brushing habit. A 1-day trial for a supplement is meaningless. Match trial length to benefit timeline. |

## Section 7: Anti-Pattern Detection

Scan the copy for patterns that Hopkins explicitly REJECTED.
Any detection is a flag for revision.

### 7A. Generality Detection

| # | Flag | Found? | Location | Fix Required |
|---|------|--------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Round numbers ("100", "50%", "thousands") | [ ] | | Replace with exact figures |
| 2 | Unnamed authorities ("experts say", "studies show") | [ ] | | Name the expert/study |
| 3 | Vague time references ("recently", "soon", "quickly") | [ ] | | Add specific dates/durations |
| 4 | Superlatives without evidence ("best", "greatest", "finest") | [ ] | | Add proof or remove |
| 5 | Unnamed beneficiaries ("customers", "people", "users") | [ ] | | Name at least one specific person |
| 6 | Vague benefits ("improved results", "better performance") | [ ] | | Quantify the improvement |
| 7 | Process vagueness ("advanced technology", "proprietary method") | [ ] | | Describe the actual process |

### 7B. Self-Praise Detection

| # | Flag | Found? | Location | Fix Required |
|---|------|--------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | "We are the best/leading/premier" | [ ] | | Show, don't tell |
| 2 | Award/ranking claims without context | [ ] | | Add verifiable details or remove |
| 3 | Self-congratulatory language | [ ] | | Replace with customer results |
| 4 | "Our" appears more than "your" | [ ] | | Rewrite from customer perspective |
| 5 | Company story before customer benefit | [ ] | | Lead with customer benefit |

### 7C. Entertainment Over Selling Detection

| # | Flag | Found? | Location | Fix Required |
|---|------|--------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Humor used as persuasion technique | [ ] | | Replace with reason-why proof |
| 2 | Clever wordplay that obscures meaning | [ ] | | Clarify with plain language |
| 3 | Story sections that do not advance the sale | [ ] | | Cut or connect to benefit |
| 4 | "Creative" formatting that reduces readability | [ ] | | Simplify for clarity |
| 5 | Pop culture references for engagement | [ ] | | Replace with relevant proof/story |

### 7D. Negative Advertising Detection

| # | Flag | Found? | Location | Fix Required |
|---|------|--------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Direct attacks on named competitors | [ ] | | Remove or preempt instead |
| 2 | Negative comparison ("unlike Brand X") | [ ] | | State your positive claim only |
| 3 | Disparaging language about alternative solutions | [ ] | | Explain why YOUR solution works |
| 4 | Fear-based competitor bashing | [ ] | | Redirect to your solution's benefits |
| 5 | Implicit negativity ("finally, someone who actually...") | [ ] | | Reframe as positive service |

### 7E. Guessing Without Testing Detection

| # | Flag | Found? | Location | Fix Required |
|---|------|--------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Only one headline version exists | [ ] | | Write 5+ variations |
| 2 | No test plan documented | [ ] | | Create test plan before launch |
| 3 | Success metrics undefined | [ ] | | Define metrics before launch |
| 4 | "We think this will work" language in notes | [ ] | | Replace with test hypothesis |
| 5 | No tracking parameters on CTAs | [ ] | | Add UTMs/key-codes |

## Section 9: Hopkins Quick-Reference Principles

Use these as rapid validation during production, before running
the full audit.

### The 10 Hopkins Commandments

| # | Commandment | Quick Test |
|---|-------------|------------|
| 1 | **The only purpose of advertising is to make sales.** | Does this copy's success metric = sales? |
| 2 | **Advertising is salesmanship. Its principles are the principles of salesmanship.** | Would a door-to-door salesman say this to a prospect? |
| 3 | **Almost any question can be answered by a test campaign.** | Is there a test plan? |
| 4 | **The more you tell, the more you sell.** | Is the story complete? All objections answered? |
| 5 | **Specificity always outperforms generality.** | Can every vague claim be made more specific? |
| 6 | **The best ads ask no one to buy.** | Does the ad serve before it sells? |
| 7 | **People can be coaxed but not driven.** | Does the tone feel like advice or pressure? |
| 8 | **Cheapness is not an appeal.** | Is the price justified by value, not discounted? |
| 9 | **Negative advertising is never done by a competent man.** | Are competitors attacked or preempted? |
| 10 | **The product itself is its own best salesman.** | Is there a trial, sample, or demonstration? |

### The 5 Fatal Sins (Instant Fail)

Any ONE of these present = automatic REWRITE, regardless of score.

| # | Sin | Why Fatal |
|---|-----|-----------|
| 1 | **No tracking/measurement** | Copy without measurement is gambling, not advertising. |
| 2 | **Zero specificity** (all claims general) | General copy is interchangeable. Interchangeable copy has no value. |
| 3 | **No reason-why** (claims without explanation) | A claim without a reason is a claim without power. |
| 4 | **Self-praise instead of proof** | "We're the best" convinces no one. Demonstration convinces everyone. |
| 5 | **Entertainment prioritized over selling** | "People do not buy from clowns." |
