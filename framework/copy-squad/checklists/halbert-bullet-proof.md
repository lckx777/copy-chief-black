## Section 1: Bullet Quality Test (10-Point)

Every bullet must pass ALL checks. Minimum: 8/10 per bullet.
Run this checklist per INDIVIDUAL bullet, then score the full set.

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | [ ] **Information Gap Created** -- The bullet hints at something specific but withholds the KEY piece. Reader must buy to close the loop. | | A bullet that reveals everything has zero selling power. The value lives in what you DON'T say. If the reader can guess the answer from the bullet alone, it fails. |
| 2 | [ ] **Standalone Desire** -- This single bullet creates enough desire to justify purchasing the ENTIRE product. Would someone buy for THIS BULLET ALONE? | | Halbert's gold standard. A bullet that is "interesting but not enough to buy for" is a weak bullet. Each fascination must be a mini-ad for the whole product. |
| 3 | [ ] **Non-Round Number Present** -- Contains at least one specific non-round number (87, not 90; $347, not $350; 23 lbs, not 25 lbs). | | Round numbers feel invented. Non-round numbers feel measured. The brain is wired to detect approximation and distrust it. |
| 4 | [ ] **Named Person or Place** -- A specific name, city, occupation, or institution appears. Not "a doctor" but "Dr. Rinaldi from Mount Sinai." | | Anonymous claims have zero credibility. Named claims feel like stories from the real world. Specificity shifts the brain from "sales pitch" to "someone's experience." |
| 5 | [ ] **Page Reference** -- References a specific page, chapter, or section (e.g., "See page 47"). | | Page references imply a REAL product with real content. They make the product tangible before purchase and imply substance. |
| 6 | [ ] **Emotional Charge** -- The bullet activates at least one of: desire, fear, outrage, hope, curiosity, vindication, relief. You can NAME which emotion. | | Emotion unnamed is emotion uncontrolled. A bullet operating on logic alone does not sell. You must know which lever you are pulling. |
| 7 | [ ] **Vivid Irrelevant Detail** -- Contains at least one sensory or circumstantial detail that isn't strictly necessary but makes it feel REAL ("at 3 AM", "wearing a red flannel shirt"). | | Nobody invents irrelevant specifics. The brain knows this instinctively. The detail that DOESN'T matter is the detail that PROVES it happened. |
| 8 | [ ] **2-3 Lines Maximum** -- The bullet does not exceed 3 lines of text. Tight, punchy, powerful. | | Long bullets lose their punch. A bullet is a JAB, not a haymaker. The moment it feels like a paragraph, it stops being a fascination. |
| 9 | [ ] **No Feature Language** -- Zero instances of: "Includes chapter on...", "Learn how to...", "Information about...", "Tips for...", "Advice on..." | | These structures DESCRIBE the product instead of SELLING it. Description is the enemy of desire. Halbert bullets never told you what was inside -- they made you DESPERATE to find out. |
| 10 | [ ] **Logo Test** -- No competitor in this niche could use this bullet without modification. It contains details proprietary to THIS offer. | | A bullet that any competitor could steal is generic. Halbert's bullets were anchored to specific mechanisms, names, and stories that belonged to the offer. Swap the product name -- if the bullet still works, it's not specific enough. |

### Bullet Set Checks (run AFTER scoring individual bullets)

| # | Check | Pass? | Why It Matters |
|---|-------|-------|----------------|
| S1 | [ ] **Strongest First, Second-Strongest Last** -- Most powerful bullet positioned FIRST (primacy), second-most-powerful LAST (recency). | | Readers remember beginnings and endings. A weak opener loses the reader before they reach the strong bullets. |
| S2 | [ ] **Minimum 3 of 7 Types** -- The set includes at least 3 different types: Curiosity, Contrarian, Story, Warning, Secret, Number, Question. | | Same-type bullets create monotony. Variety keeps each bullet fresh and activates different psychological levers. |
| S3 | [ ] **Opening Word Variety** -- No two consecutive bullets start with the same word or structure (not "The... The... The..."). | | Repetitive openers create visual monotony. Each bullet must look DIFFERENT at first glance to earn individual attention. |
| S4 | [ ] **Read-Aloud Test** -- Every bullet read aloud. Any stumble = rewrite. | | The ear catches what the eye misses. Stumbling kills momentum. Halbert read everything aloud. |
| S5 | [ ] **"So What?" Test** -- For each bullet, ask "So what?" If no immediate visceral answer, add stakes. | | Curiosity without stakes is trivia. Curiosity WITH stakes is urgency. |

### Bullet Scoring

| Individual Score | Set Score | Rating | Action |
|-----------------|-----------|--------|--------|
| 9-10 / bullet | 5/5 set | HALBERT-GRADE | Ship with confidence |
| 8 / bullet | 4/5 set | STRONG | Address gaps, then ship |
| 6-7 / bullet | 3/5 set | ADEQUATE | Rewrite weakest bullets |
| 4-5 / bullet | 2/5 set | WEAK | Major rewrite required |
| Below 4 | Below 2 | REJECT | Start over (see halbert.md F3) |

## Section 2: Lead Opening Formulas (5 Types)

Every Halbert lead follows the 3-Step architecture: (1) Big claim,
(2) Quick credibility, (3) Story transition. These 5 types are
variations on Step 1 flavor.

### Type A: The Shock Lead

**When to use:** Health, finance, anything where a startling fact creates urgency.

**Formula:**
```
[Shocking statistic or claim] -- [one-line credibility anchor] --
[transition to a specific person's story]
```

**Example:**
```
There are 43 million men in America walking around with a ticking time
bomb in their chest. And most of them have no idea.

My name is Dr. Klaus Richter, and for 17 years at the University of
Heidelberg I studied the one thing cardiologists never talk about.

It started when a 52-year-old plumber named Hans Gruber from Munich
walked into my clinic and said four words that changed everything...
```

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Opening statistic is SPECIFIC (non-round number)
- [ ] Statistic creates fear, outrage, or urgency
- [ ] Credibility anchor in sentence 2-3 (name + institution + years)
- [ ] Story transition uses a NAMED person with occupation and location
- [ ] Total lead is 5-7 sentences maximum
- [ ] First sentence under 15 words

### Type B: The Story Lead

**When to use:** Testimonial-driven offers, relatable avatar situations.

**Formula:**
```
[Vivid scene-setting with time/place] -- [the moment everything changed] --
[quick credibility why this matters]
```

**Example:**
```
It was 3:17 AM on a Tuesday when Maria Fernandes woke up drenched in
sweat for the 47th night in a row.

She looked at the clock. Looked at her husband sleeping peacefully.
And made a decision that -- 87 days later -- her doctor would call
"the most remarkable recovery I've seen in 23 years of practice."

What she did that night was so simple it almost sounds stupid...
```

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Opens with a specific TIME and PLACE (not "one day" or "recently")
- [ ] Named character with at least 2 identifying details
- [ ] Emotional moment is SHOWN, not told
- [ ] Bridge to credibility happens naturally within the story
- [ ] Creates a gap: reader MUST know what happens next
- [ ] First sentence under 20 words

### Type C: The Question Lead

**When to use:** Relationship, self-improvement, any personal/sensitive topic.

**Formula:**
```
[Personal question that the reader cannot NOT answer mentally] --
[follow-up that reframes the question] -- [promise of the answer]
```

**Example:**
```
Can I ask you something personal?

When was the last time you looked in the mirror and actually LIKED
what you saw? Not tolerated. Not accepted. LIKED.

If it's been more than 6 months, what I'm about to share could change
that -- starting in the next 14 days. And it has nothing to do with
diet, exercise, or willpower.
```

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Opening question is genuinely PERSONAL (reader answers in their head)
- [ ] Question relates to the core DRE of the avatar
- [ ] Follow-up sentence deepens the emotional cut
- [ ] Promise is SPECIFIC (14 days, not "soon")
- [ ] Promise eliminates the obvious wrong answers
- [ ] Total lead is 5-7 sentences

### Type D: The Warning Lead

**When to use:** Competitive markets, saturated niches where the reader is skeptical.

**Formula:**
```
[WARNING about what NOT to do] -- [specific reason why] --
[transition to what TO do instead]
```

**Example:**
```
Whatever you do, do NOT buy another prostate supplement until you
read the next 3 paragraphs.

Because according to a study published by researchers at Johns Hopkins
last March, 7 out of the 10 best-selling prostate formulas contain an
ingredient that -- at doses above 40mg -- actually makes the problem
WORSE. Not better. Worse.

And there's a 73% chance the one sitting in your medicine cabinet
right now is one of them.
```

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Opens with a direct WARNING that creates immediate stakes
- [ ] Warning is SPECIFIC (not "be careful" but "do NOT do X")
- [ ] Credibility anchor uses specific source (institution, publication, date)
- [ ] Percentage or number makes the threat PERSONAL to the reader
- [ ] Creates paranoia/urgency that can only be resolved by reading further
- [ ] Total lead is 5-7 sentences

### Type E: The Secret Lead

**When to use:** Insider/exclusivity angle, discovery-based mechanisms.

**Formula:**
```
[What you're about to learn was never meant for the public] --
[who has been keeping it secret and why] -- [how you got access]
```

**Example:**
```
What I'm about to share with you was never supposed to leave the
4th floor of Heidelberg University Medical Center.

It's a protocol that's been used quietly -- almost secretly -- by
a small group of European ENT specialists since 2019. The reason
you've never heard of it? It would make $3.2 billion in hearing
aid revenue disappear overnight.

A colleague of mine smuggled out the research last November. And
now, for the first time, I can show you exactly what they found...
```

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Opens with exclusivity signal (this was hidden/secret)
- [ ] Names SPECIFIC institutions, locations, dates
- [ ] Explains WHO has been hiding it and WHY (follow the money)
- [ ] The "how you got access" element feels like insider betrayal
- [ ] Creates conspiracy curiosity: reader feels they're getting forbidden info
- [ ] Total lead is 5-7 sentences

## Section 3: P.S. Structure Checklist

Every Halbert letter includes 2-3 P.S. lines. The P.S. is the second
most-read element after the headline. These checks apply to EACH P.S. line.

| # | Check | Pass? | Details |
|---|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | [ ] **Standalone readability** -- The P.S. makes sense WITHOUT reading the letter body. A reader who jumps from headline to P.S. gets a complete mini-pitch. | | The P.S. is often read BEFORE the body. It must function independently. |
| 2 | [ ] **Contains a specific number** -- At least one non-round number appears (a date, a dollar amount, a quantity, a timeframe). | | Specificity in the P.S. anchors the urgency or benefit as REAL. |
| 3 | [ ] **P.S. #1 restates the main benefit** -- The first P.S. reminds the reader of the SINGLE most important thing they gain. | | After reading the full letter, the reader may have forgotten the core promise. P.S. #1 re-anchors it. |
| 4 | [ ] **P.P.S. adds urgency with deadline** -- The second P.S. introduces a SPECIFIC deadline or scarcity element. Not "limited time" but "midnight Friday, March 14th." | | Vague urgency creates vague action. Specific deadlines create specific action (now). |
| 5 | [ ] **P.P.P.S. introduces a surprise** -- The third P.S. adds something NOT mentioned in the letter body (a bonus, a guarantee sweetener, a personal note). | | Surprise in the P.S. creates a feeling of hidden value -- as if the letter keeps giving even AFTER it ends. |
| 6 | [ ] **No P.S. exceeds 3-4 sentences** -- Each P.S. is tight and punchy. Not a second letter within the letter. | | A long P.S. loses its punch. The power is in brevity and surprise. |
| 7 | [ ] **P.S. tone matches letter tone** -- The P.S. sounds like the same person who wrote the letter, not a different voice or register. | | A tonal shift in the P.S. breaks the spell of intimacy the letter created. |

### P.S. Templates

**P.S. (Benefit Restate):**
```
P.S. -- Remember: [SPECIFIC PERSON] used this exact [METHOD NAME]
to [SPECIFIC RESULT] in [SPECIFIC TIMEFRAME]. And the same [METHOD]
is waiting for you on page [X] of [PRODUCT NAME]. But only if you
[ACTION] before [SPECIFIC DEADLINE].
```

**P.P.S. (Urgency/Deadline):**
```
P.P.S. -- I can only guarantee this [PRICE/AVAILABILITY] until
[SPECIFIC DATE AND TIME]. After that, [SPECIFIC CONSEQUENCE].
This is not a "marketing tactic" -- [HONEST REASON FOR DEADLINE].
```

**P.P.P.S. (Surprise Bonus):**
```
P.P.P.S. -- I almost forgot. Everyone who [ACTIONS] by [DATE]
will also receive [SURPRISE BONUS] -- a [$VALUE] [DESCRIPTION]
that I originally created for [EXCLUSIVE GROUP]. I've never
offered this to the public before.
```

## Section 4: A-Pile Envelope Checklist

For physical direct mail OR the digital equivalent (email, ad, DM).
The goal: make the communication FEEL like personal correspondence,
not marketing.

### Physical Mail (Direct Mail)

| # | Check | Pass? | Details |
|---|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | [ ] **Hand-addressed** -- Envelope addressed in handwriting (or handwriting font that looks authentic). Blue or black ink. Slightly messy. | | Machine-printed labels = B-pile instant identification. Handwriting signals a personal sender. |
| 2 | [ ] **Real stamp** -- A real postage stamp, not metered or bulk postage. Ideally a commemorative stamp (creates visual interest). | | Metered postage screams "mass mail." Real stamps signal that a human went to the post office. |
| 3 | [ ] **No return address** (or personal-sounding return address) -- No company logo, no business name. Either blank or "J. Smith, 123 Oak Lane, Topeka, KS" | | A company return address triggers immediate categorization as marketing. A personal address (or no address) creates curiosity. |
| 4 | [ ] **Non-standard envelope** -- Not the standard #10 white business envelope. Use 6x9, invitation-size, colored, textured, or anything unusual. | | Standard envelopes look like standard mail. Unusual envelopes look like something PERSONAL or SPECIAL. |
| 5 | [ ] **Lumpy mail** -- The envelope contains something that creates a lump (coin, seed packet, small object). The recipient FEELS it before opening. | | A lumpy envelope is almost impossible to throw away without opening. The physical curiosity is overwhelming. |
| 6 | [ ] **No teaser copy on envelope** -- The outside of the envelope says NOTHING about what's inside (no "FREE GIFT INSIDE!" no "URGENT NOTICE"). | | Teaser copy on the envelope is the #1 indicator of marketing mail. A blank or hand-addressed envelope with no teaser = A-pile. |
| 7 | [ ] **First name only addressing** -- "John" or "John Smith" not "Mr. John T. Smith" or "Dear Valued Customer." | | Formal addressing signals business correspondence. First-name addressing signals personal correspondence. |

### Digital (Email / DM / Ad)

| # | Check | Pass? | Details |
|---|-------|-------|---------|
| D1 | [ ] **Subject line sounds personal** -- Reads like it came from a friend, not a newsletter. Lowercase is acceptable. No exclamation marks. No ALL CAPS. | | "re: that thing I mentioned" beats "EXCLUSIVE OFFER -- 50% OFF!!!" |
| D2 | [ ] **Sender is a person, not a brand** -- "Klaus" or "Klaus Richter" not "Neuvelys Team" or "support@company.com" | | People open emails from PEOPLE. They delete emails from brands. |
| D3 | [ ] **Plain text or minimal formatting** -- No banners, no headers, no colored buttons, no column layouts. Just text. Like a real email. | | HTML templates signal "marketing email." Plain text signals "someone sat down and wrote me." |
| D4 | [ ] **Conversational opening** -- First line reads like a mid-conversation continuation, not a formal greeting. | | "So I was thinking about what you said..." beats "Dear Subscriber, We are pleased to announce..." |
| D5 | [ ] **Sent at an irregular time** -- Not 9:00 AM or 12:00 PM sharp. Sent at 10:47 AM or 2:13 PM. | | Marketing emails are scheduled in batch. Personal emails are sent when the person WRITES them. |

## Section 5: Physical Grabber Ideas List

Organized by niche relevance. Each grabber must CONNECT to the message
and be acknowledged in the first 2-3 sentences of the letter.

### Universal Grabbers (Any Niche)

| Grabber | Connection Phrase | Best For |
|---------|------------------|----------|
| Dollar bill | "I attached real money because what I'm about to share is about MONEY..." | Finance, business, any ROI-focused offer |
| Penny (old/unusual) | "This penny is from [YEAR] -- the same year [EVENT] happened that changed everything about [TOPIC]..." | History-based mechanisms, discovery stories |
| Handwritten note on a Post-It | "I know this is unusual, but I needed to tell you something important..." | Personal, intimate, relationship offers |
| Tea bag | "Before you read this letter, I want you to make yourself a cup of tea. What I'm about to share deserves your full, relaxed attention..." | Health, wellness, relaxation |
| Packet of seeds | "Inside this envelope you'll find seeds. Not just any seeds -- the same [SPECIES] that [CREDENTIALED PERSON] discovered [BENEFIT]..." | Growth, transformation, health |

### Health / Supplement Niche

| Grabber | Connection Phrase | Best For |
|---------|------------------|----------|
| Salt packet | "This tiny packet of salt contains the same mineral compound that [SPECIFIC DISCOVERY]..." | Mineral/electrolyte mechanisms |
| Magnifying glass (flat, plastic) | "Hold this up to the ingredient list on your current supplement. What you're about to see will shock you..." | Exposing competitor products |
| Band-Aid | "You've been putting a Band-Aid on [PROBLEM]. It's time for the real fix..." | Pain, chronic conditions |
| Small measuring spoon | "This is the exact dose of [INGREDIENT] that [RESULT]. No more. No less." | Dosage-specific mechanisms |

### Concursos / Education Niche

## Quick Reference: The 7 Bullet Types

| Type | Pattern | Key Lever | Example Starter |
|------|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Curiosity** | "The [specific thing] that [surprising result]" | Information gap | "The $3 ingredient..." |
| **Contrarian** | "Why [common belief] is [wrong/dangerous]" | Indignation | "Why everything your doctor told you..." |
| **Story** | "How [specific person] [achieved result]" | Relatability | "How a retired plumber from Akron..." |
| **Warning** | "The [danger] that [consequence]" | Fear + urgency | "WARNING: The one food that..." |
| **Secret** | "The [insider method] [insiders] use" | Exclusivity | "The trick Japanese centenarians..." |
| **Number** | "[Exact number] [things] to [benefit]" | Completeness | "The 7 minerals missing from..." |
| **Question** | "What [unexpected thing] reveals about [you]" | Self-diagnosis | "Can you pass this 10-second test?" |
