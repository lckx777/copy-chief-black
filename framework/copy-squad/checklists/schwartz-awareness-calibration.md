# Schwartz Awareness + Sophistication Calibration Checklist

> **Expert:** Eugene Schwartz
> **Source:** Breakthrough Advertising (1966) + Voice DNA Profile
> **Use:** MANDATORY diagnostic before writing ANY headline, lead, opening, VSL hook, LP hero, or criativo.
> **Rule:** No copy is produced until this checklist is completed and the strategic diagnosis is documented.

---

## PART 1: Diagnose Awareness Level

Answer these 5 questions to determine where your prospect sits on the
awareness spectrum. Be honest. When in doubt, choose ONE LEVEL LOWER
than you think -- it is always safer to meet them where they are than
to overshoot.

### Diagnostic Questions

- [ ] **Q1: Does the prospect know OUR specific product by name?**
  - YES and ready to buy → **MOST AWARE** (Level 5)
  - YES but evaluating → **PRODUCT AWARE** (Level 4)
  - NO → Continue to Q2

- [ ] **Q2: Does the prospect know that solutions to their problem exist?**
  - YES, and they've tried some → **SOLUTION AWARE** (Level 3)
  - YES, but haven't tried any → **SOLUTION AWARE** (Level 3)
  - NO → Continue to Q3

- [ ] **Q3: Does the prospect know they HAVE the problem?**
  - YES, actively suffering → **PROBLEM AWARE** (Level 2)
  - YES, but not urgent → **PROBLEM AWARE** (Level 2, low intensity)
  - NO → **UNAWARE** (Level 1)

- [ ] **Q4: What is the prospect actively SEARCHING for?** (VOC validation)
  - "Where to buy [product]?" / "Discount code for [product]?" → Level 5
  - "Has anyone tried [product]?" / "Is [product] legit?" → Level 4
  - "What's the best [solution type]?" / "Which [solution] works?" → Level 3
  - "Why does [symptom] happen?" / "Is [symptom] normal?" → Level 2
  - No searches related to problem → Level 1

- [ ] **Q5: What does the prospect's VOC language reveal?**
  - Mentions your product name → Level 4-5
  - Mentions competitor products or solution categories → Level 3
  - Describes symptoms and frustrations without naming solutions → Level 2
  - Does not discuss the problem at all → Level 1

**DIAGNOSED AWARENESS LEVEL: _____________**

## PART 2: Diagnose Sophistication Level

Answer these 5 questions to determine how EXPOSED this market is to
promises like yours. Sophistication is about exposure, not intelligence.
When in doubt, choose ONE LEVEL HIGHER than you think -- markets are
almost always more jaded than copywriters assume.

### Diagnostic Questions

- [ ] **Q1: How many competitors are making SIMILAR promises?**
  - None or 1-2 → **FIRST** (Level 1)
  - 3-5 → **SECOND** (Level 2)
  - 6-10 → **THIRD** (Level 3)
  - 10-20 → **FOURTH** (Level 4)
  - 20+ / entire market makes this promise → **FIFTH** (Level 5)

- [ ] **Q2: Are competitors using MECHANISMS in their headlines?**
  - No, just claims and promises → Level 1-2
  - Some are, most aren't → Level 3 (emerging)
  - Most are naming mechanisms → Level 3-4
  - Multiple mechanisms competing against each other → Level 4-5

- [ ] **Q3: What is the market's reaction to new claims?**
  - Excitement and curiosity → Level 1-2
  - Interest but skepticism → Level 3
  - "I've heard this before" → Level 4
  - "All marketing is BS" / active distrust → Level 5

- [ ] **Q4: What does the Ads Library Spy reveal?**
  - Few competitors running ads → Level 1-2
  - Many competitors, similar claims → Level 3
  - Many competitors, mechanism-based copy → Level 4
  - Long-running ads are story/identity-based → Level 5
  - Scale Scores 20+ for multiple competitors → Level 4-5

- [ ] **Q5: What has the prospect already TRIED and FAILED with?**
  - Nothing → Level 1
  - 1-2 generic solutions → Level 2
  - Multiple solutions including mechanism-based ones → Level 3-4
  - "I've tried everything and nothing works" → Level 4-5
  - Actively hostile toward marketing claims → Level 5

**DIAGNOSED SOPHISTICATION LEVEL: _____________**

## PART 3: Strategic Diagnosis Statement

Fill in this template BEFORE writing any copy:

```
STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS:
- Awareness Level: [LEVEL] — My prospect is [DESCRIPTION]
- Sophistication Level: [LEVEL] — This market has [DESCRIPTION]
- Mass Desire: [What thousands already want]
- Desire Trifecta:
  - Core (stated): [What they openly want]
  - Secondary (consequence): [What achieving core gives them]
  - Hidden (unspoken): [What they won't admit they want]
- My headline will: [ENTER their conversation about X]
- My headline leads with: [CLAIM / MECHANISM / PROOF / IDENTIFICATION / DEAL]
- My opening advances them from: [CURRENT LEVEL] → [NEXT LEVEL]
```

## PART 4: Headline Formula Per Awareness Level

### Level 1 — UNAWARE

```
GOAL: Create curiosity without naming the problem.
ENTRY POINT: Universal human interest (story, shock, intrigue).
HEADLINE FORMULA: "[Unexpected character] + [unexpected action] + [implied benefit]"

OPENING STRATEGY:
  - Start with a STORY or a provocative statement
  - Name NO product, NO solution, NO problem
  - Build curiosity for 200-500 words before revealing the problem
  - Let the reader DISCOVER the problem through the narrative

PROOF REQUIREMENTS:
  - Proof is woven into the story naturally
  - Authority enters as a CHARACTER, not a credential
  - Documentation appears as "discovery," not "study"

CTA STYLE:
  - Delayed. Comes only AFTER full awareness progression.
  - Soft: "If this sounds like something worth exploring..."
  - Never direct at this stage.

EXAMPLE HEADLINES:
  "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano -- But When I Started to Play..."
  "The Doctor's Wife Who Never Gets Sick"
  "How a Strange Accident in a Heidelberg Lab Changed 47 Million Lives"
  "What This 73-Year-Old Fisherman Knows About Memory That No Doctor Will Tell You"
```

### Level 2 — PROBLEM AWARE

```
GOAL: Validate the problem. Empathize. Promise explanation.
ENTRY POINT: The SYMPTOM the prospect is experiencing right now.
HEADLINE FORMULA: "If you [specific symptom], here's [explanation/reason]"

OPENING STRATEGY:
  - Name the symptom in the FIRST sentence (specific, sensory)
  - Empathize: "You're not alone. And it's not your fault."
  - Explain WHY the problem exists (introduce mechanism)
  - Move them toward Solution Aware within the first 500 words

PROOF REQUIREMENTS:
  - Statistics about HOW COMMON the problem is
  - Explanation of the MECHANISM behind the problem
  - Authority who explains the "real cause"

CTA STYLE:
  - Transitional: "Keep reading to discover what actually works..."
  - Educational tone, not salesy
  - The prospect needs UNDERSTANDING before they need a product

EXAMPLE HEADLINES:
  "If You Wake Up at 3AM With a Racing Heart, Read This"
  "Why Your Knees Hurt MORE After Exercise (Not Less)"
  "That Ringing in Your Ears? It's Not What Your Doctor Told You"
  "Tired of Studying 12 Hours a Day and Still Failing the Same Questions?"
  "When Nothing Works and You Start to Wonder If the Problem Is YOU"
```

### Level 3 — SOLUTION AWARE

```
GOAL: Differentiate your MECHANISM from all other solutions.
ENTRY POINT: The prospect's FRUSTRATION with solutions that failed.
HEADLINE FORMULA: "The [mechanism name] approach to [desire] — [differentiator]"

OPENING STRATEGY:
  - Acknowledge solutions they've already tried
  - Explain WHY those solutions failed (they addressed symptom, not cause)
  - Introduce YOUR mechanism as fundamentally different
  - The mechanism IS the story at this level

PROOF REQUIREMENTS:
  - Mechanism of action explanation (HOW it works)
  - Differentiation from competing solutions (WHY theirs failed)
  - Authority who validates the mechanism
  - Documentation or demonstration of the mechanism

CTA STYLE:
  - Mechanism-driven: "Discover how [mechanism] works for you"
  - Specificity: include timeframe and expected result
  - Bridge: from understanding mechanism to trying product

EXAMPLE HEADLINES:
  "The 'Otolith Crystal' Method That Silences Tinnitus in 21 Days"
  "Forget Diets. This Enzyme Does What Willpower Never Could."
  "The Real Reason 94% of Concurseiros Fail (It's Not Study Hours)"
  "How [Mechanism Name] Reverses [Problem] Without [Common Approach]"
  "The Hidden Pattern Inside Every Banca Examinadora -- And How to Exploit It"
```

### Level 4 — PRODUCT AWARE

```
GOAL: Prove your specific claims. Remove remaining doubt.
ENTRY POINT: The prospect's EVALUATION of your product.
HEADLINE FORMULA: "[Product] + [specific result] + [proof element]"

OPENING STRATEGY:
  - Lead with PROOF, not promises (they've heard promises)
  - Specific results: numbers, names, timeframes
  - Address specific objections head-on
  - Social proof heavy: who else uses it and what happened

PROOF REQUIREMENTS:
  - DEMONSTRATION if possible (video, before/after)
  - DOCUMENTATION (studies, published data, patents)
  - TESTIMONIALS with full specificity (name, location, result, timeframe)
  - GUARANTEE details (removes remaining risk)

CTA STYLE:
  - Direct: "Try [Product] risk-free for [timeframe]"
  - Urgency: limited supply, deadline, bonus expiring
  - Risk reversal: guarantee prominently featured

EXAMPLE HEADLINES:
  "Neuvelys: The Formula 12,847 Europeans Use to Silence Their Ears"
  "Why 87.3% of [Product] Users Report [Result] Within 21 Days"
  "[Product]: Backed by [Authority], Proven in [Study], Guaranteed for [Timeframe]"
  "What Happens When [Number] People Try [Product] — The Data Will Surprise You"
```

### Level 5 — MOST AWARE

```
GOAL: Close the sale. Give the deal. Get out of the way.
ENTRY POINT: The prospect's READINESS to buy.
HEADLINE FORMULA: "[Offer] + [urgency/scarcity]"

OPENING STRATEGY:
  - DO NOT re-sell. They already believe.
  - DO NOT re-educate. They already understand.
  - Lead with the OFFER: price, terms, bonuses, guarantee
  - Create urgency: deadline, limited quantity, expiring bonus

PROOF REQUIREMENTS:
  - Minimal. They already trust. Over-proving creates doubt.
  - Social proof as reinforcement, not persuasion
  - Guarantee as final risk-removal, not proof

CTA STYLE:
  - Immediate and direct: "Click below to order now"
  - Urgency: "This offer expires at midnight"
  - Scarcity: "Only [X] units at this price"
  - NO preamble. NO warm-up. DEAL → CTA.

EXAMPLE HEADLINES:
  "Save 40% on [Product] — This Week Only"
  "3 Bottles for the Price of 2 (Until Friday)"
  "Last Chance: [Product] at the Lowest Price We've Ever Offered"
  "[Product] — Order Before Midnight, Get [Bonus] Free"
```

## PART 5: Common Mistakes Per Level

### Mistakes with UNAWARE Prospects

- [ ] Naming the problem in the headline (they don't know they have it)
- [ ] Mentioning the product too early (they don't care yet)
- [ ] Using mechanism language (they don't know what a mechanism is)
- [ ] Being too direct (they need to be DRAWN IN, not pushed)
- [ ] Writing a headline that only appeals to problem-aware people

### Mistakes with PROBLEM AWARE Prospects

- [ ] Jumping straight to the solution (they need empathy first)
- [ ] Using generic symptom descriptions ("if you're unhappy")
- [ ] Skipping the mechanism explanation (they need to understand WHY)
- [ ] Making it about the product before making it about THEM
- [ ] Not validating their frustration with failed attempts

### Mistakes with SOLUTION AWARE Prospects

- [ ] Leading with the product instead of the mechanism
- [ ] Not differentiating from solutions they've already tried
- [ ] Using the same language as competing solutions
- [ ] Failing to explain WHY previous solutions failed
- [ ] Making claims without mechanism backing

### Mistakes with PRODUCT AWARE Prospects

- [ ] Re-educating instead of proving (they know the problem already)
- [ ] Leading with promises instead of proof
- [ ] Using vague testimonials ("Great product!" -- J.S.)
- [ ] Not addressing specific objections they have
- [ ] Forgetting the guarantee (remaining risk = no purchase)

### Mistakes with MOST AWARE Prospects

- [ ] Re-selling to people who already want to buy
- [ ] Long copy when short copy + deal would convert better
- [ ] Not including urgency or scarcity (no reason to act NOW)
- [ ] Burying the offer under unnecessary copy
- [ ] Adding copy that creates NEW objections

## PART 6: Sophistication-Specific Headline Adjustments

| Sophistication | Headline Must Include | Headline Must Avoid |
|---------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Level 1** | Direct claim. Bold promise. | Mechanism (unnecessary). Hedging. |
| **Level 2** | Expanded claim. Added dimension. | Same claim as Level 1. Timidity. |
| **Level 3** | Mechanism name. How it works. | Claim alone (not enough). Generic mechanism. |
| **Level 4** | Mechanism + authority + specificity. | Mechanism alone (not enough). Round numbers. |
| **Level 5** | Identification. Story. No claims. | Any direct claim (instant distrust). Product name in headline. |

## PART 7: Quick Calibration Card

```
STEP 1: AWARENESS
  Most Aware (5) ←→ Product (4) ←→ Solution (3) ←→ Problem (2) ←→ Unaware (1)
  RULE: When in doubt, aim ONE LEVEL LOWER.

STEP 2: SOPHISTICATION
  First (1) ←→ Second (2) ←→ Third (3) ←→ Fourth (4) ←→ Fifth (5)
  RULE: When in doubt, aim ONE LEVEL HIGHER.

STEP 3: LEAD WITH...
  Soph 1-2: THE CLAIM (promise, result, benefit)
  Soph 3:   THE MECHANISM (how, why, the new approach)
  Soph 4:   THE PROOF (documentation, authority, specificity)
  Soph 5:   THE IDENTITY (story, recognition, "you are not alone")

STEP 4: DESIRE
  Primary (headline) + Secondary (body) + Hidden (subtext, NEVER stated)

STEP 5: WRITE
  First 50 words must: GRAB + IDENTIFY + PROMISE + CREATE MOMENTUM
```

## PART 8: Pre-Production Validation

Before ANY copy goes to production, verify:

- [ ] Awareness level is DIAGNOSED and DOCUMENTED (not assumed)
- [ ] Sophistication level is DIAGNOSED and DOCUMENTED (not guessed)
- [ ] Mass desire is identified from VOC data (not invented)
- [ ] Desire trifecta is mapped (core, secondary, hidden)
- [ ] Strategic diagnosis statement is complete (Part 3)
- [ ] Headline matches the awareness-sophistication intersection
- [ ] First 50 words accomplish all four functions
- [ ] Proof is stacked from multiple levels of the hierarchy
- [ ] Hidden desire is implied, never stated directly
- [ ] Headline could NOT be used by a competitor without changes
- [ ] Body copy advances awareness progressively (one level at a time)

**IF ANY CHECKBOX IS UNCHECKED: Do not proceed to production.**
